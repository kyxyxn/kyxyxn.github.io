Many studies aim to identify the causal impact of treatments or interventions on various outcomes. However, executing a fully randomized experiment is often unfeasible for ethical or practical reasons, leading researchers to rely on observational studies. A primary concern in these studies is the presence of unmeasured confounding, which can compromise their validity. Variables like lab test results, clinical data, lifestyle factors, and assessments of cognitive and physical abilities are often missing from secondary databases, posing a substantial risk of bias, particularly in studies examining intended effects where a strong correlation exists between treatment decisions and outcomes.

Approaches such as propensity score analysis, regression, and matching are employed to mitigate the influence of confounders on causal effect estimates, but they only address confounders that are measurable. To adjust for those that are unmeasured, the instrumental variable (IV) method can be utilized.

## What is a valid instrument?
Informally, an Instrumental Variable (IV) is one that predicts the treatment under study but is unrelated to the outcome of the study except through its effect on the treatment. Formally, a variable must exhibit three key characteristics to qualify as an IV: (1) relevance, meaning that the IV must cause a change in the treatment received; (2) effective random assignment, signifying that the IV must be as if randomly assigned, independent of unmeasured confounders when conditioned on covariates; and (3) exclusion restriction, implying that the IV should not directly affect the outcomes.


## Monotonicity assumption and compliance classes
The three fundamental assumptions above allow us to establish an upper and lower bound of the causal effect [^1]. However, these bounds tend to be quite broad, resulting in significant uncertainty when estimating the causal effect. They also emphasize the additional information needed from a fourth assumption to achieve a precise point estimate. This fourth assumption, known as monotonicity, asserts that the instrument affects the treatment assignment consistently in the same direction for all individuals in the population. Specifically, it means there are no individuals for whom the instrument causes the opposite effect in terms of treatment compliance.

To keep things straightforward, let's examine IV estimation for a binary instrumental variable (IV) ($$Z=\{0, 1\}$$) and binary treatment ($$D=\{0, 1\}$$). In a study with a binary IV and treatment, subjects can be categorized into one of four latent compliance classes based on the joint values of treatment assignment and potential treatment received[^2]. These compliance classes are known as compliers, never-takers, always-takers, and defiers. Compliers are individuals who follow the treatment assignment (i.e., they receive the treatment when assigned). Never-takers are individuals who never take the treatment regardless of their assignment to the treatment group. Always-takers are those who take the treatment regardless of not being assigned to it. Defiers are individuals who do the opposite of the assignment.

Let $$D^z$$ be the $$N$$-dimensional vector representing the potential treatment under IV assignment $$z$$ with elements $$D^z_i, \ i=1,...,N$$. Each element $$D^z_i$$ can be either $$1$$ or $$0$$, indicating whether individual $$i$$ would receive treatment level $$1$$ or $$0$$ under IV assignment $$z$$. Specifically, $$D^0_i$$ signifies that individual $$i$$ has $$z=0$$, and $$D^1_i$$ signifies that individual $$i$$ has $$z=1$$. 

When $$D^0_i=0$$, it means that the individual $$i$$ did not receive the treatment when not assigned to the treatment gorup; $$D^0_i=1$$ indicates that they did receive the treatment under the same condition. Conversely, $$D^1_i=0$$ means that the individual $$i$$ did not receive the treatment when assigned to the treatment group, while $$D^1_i=1$$ indicates that they received treatment. 

The four compliance classes can each be represented as follows:

Compliance class | Notation 
---- | ---- |
Compliers | $$(D^0_i,D^1_i)=(0,1)$$ 
Never-takers | $$(D^0_i,D^1_i)=(0,0)$$ 
Always-takers | $$(D^0_i,D^1_i)=(1,1)$$
Defiers | $$(D^0_i,D^1_i)=(1,0)$$ 

IV analysis often focuses on estimating the **Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE)**, which is the average treatment effect for the compliers. Identifying compliers help isolate the causal effect of the treatment on this specific group, providing a more precise estimate than the overall average treatment effect (ATE), which might be biased due to the presence of never-takers, always-takers, and defiers. 


## IV and causality
Let's examine the relationship between instrumental variable $$Z$$, a treatment $$D$$, unmeasured confounders $$U$$, and an outcome $$Y$$ through a directed acyclic graph. 

![dag](/assets/images/dag-iv.png)

In this graph, an arrow from the treatment $$D$$ to the outcome $$Y$$ indicates that changing the value of $$D$$ causes a change in $$Y$$. The variable $$U$$ is a source of concern because it is causally linked to both $$Y$$ and the assignment of $$D$$. Since $$U$$ is not recorded in the data set, it cannot be directly adjusted for using statistical methods such as propensity scores or regression. 

To illustrate this with an example, Cook et al. (2010)[^3] examined the impact of nurse staffing levels ($$D$$) on patient outcomes ($$Y$$), using the implementation of minimum staffing regulations in California ($$Z$$) as an insturmental variable. The unmeasured confounders ($$U$$) in this study could include hospital management practices and patient severity of illness, which affect both staffing levels and patient outcomes. By using the regulation as an IV, the researchers aim to isolate the causal effect of nurse staffing on patient outcomes, accounting for unmeasured confounders. The regulation creates exogenous variation in nurse staffing levels across hospitals, meaning that changes in staffing levels can be attributed to the regulation rather than to other internal hospital factors or patient characteristics. Hospitals must comply with the regulation, leading to an increase in nurse staffing levels in hospitals that were previously below the mandated ratios. This satisfies the relevance condition. The regulation is also likely not correlated with the unmeasured confounders that affect patient outcomes.

Let's delve into the mechanics of IV. Suppose that the potential outcomes can be expressed as $$Y_{si} = f_i(d)$$, where $$f_i(d) = \alpha + \rho d + \eta_i$$. Imagine there is a vector of control variables $$U_i$$ that account for unobservable characteristics. We can decompose the error term $$\eta_i$$ as $$\eta_i = U'_i \gamma + \nu_i$$, where $$\gamma$$ is a vector of population regression coefficients, and $$\nu_i$$ is a random error term uncorrelated with the control variables $$U_i$$. We assume that the correlation between $$\eta_i$$ and the treatment $$d_i$$ arises solely because of the control variables $$U_i$$. This implies that $$E[d_i\nu_i] = 0$$, meaning there is no corerlation between the treatment $$d_i$$ and the residual error term $$\nu_i$$.

If the control variables $$U_i$$ were observed, we would include them in the regression of $$d$$ on $$Y$$, resulting in: $$Y_i = \alpha + \rho d_i + U'_i \gamma + \nu_i$$. In this model, the error term $$\nu_i$$ represents the random component of the potential outcomes after accounting for $$U_i$$. By construction, this error term is uncorrelated with the treatment $$d_i$$. If our assumption $$E[d_i \nu_i] = 0$$ holds, the population regression of $$Y_i$$ on $$d_i$$ and $$U_i$$ yields unbiased estimates of the coefficient $$\rho$$. 

The challenge is to estimate the long regression coefficient, $$\rho$$, when $$U_i$$ is unobserved. IV methods can help if we have access to a variable $$z_i$$ that is correlated with the causal variable of interest $$d_i$$ but uncorrelated with any other determinants of the dependent variable. This implies: $$Cov(d_i, z_i) \neq 0$$ and $$Cov(\eta_i, z_i) = 0$$. 

The IV approach leverages the properties of the instrument to isolate the exogenous variation in the treatment $$d_i$$ and use it to estimate $$\rho$$. This can be understood through the following steps:


1. First stage equation: Regress the treatment $$d_i$$ on the instrument $$z_i$$: $$d_i = \pi z_i + \epsilon_i$$. $$pi$$ is the effect of the instrument on the treatment: $$\pi = \frac{Cov(d_i, z_i)}{Var(z_i)}$$.

2. Reduced form equation: The reduced form regression captures the relationship between the instrument $$z_i$$ and the outcome $$Y_i$$: $$Y = \beta z + u$$. 

3. Structural equation: The structural model is: $$Y_i = \alpha + \rho d_i + \eta_i$$.

4. Substitute first stage into structural equation: Substitute $$d_i = \pi z_i + \epsilon_i$$ into $$Y_i = \alpha + \rho d_i + \eta_i$$: $$Y_i = \alpha + \rho (\pi z_i + \epsilon_i) + \eta_i = \alpha + \rho \pi z_i + \rho \epsilon_i + \eta_i$$

5. Compare with reduced form: By comparing $$Y_i = \alpha + \rho \pi z_i + \rho \epsilon_i + \eta_i$$ with $$Y = \beta z + u$$, we see that $$\beta = \rho \pi$$.

6. Solve for $$\rho$$: $$\rho = \frac{\beta}{\pi}$$

The coefficient $$\rho$$ can be written as $$\frac{\beta}{\pi}$$ because $$\beta$$ captures the total effect of the instrument on the outcome, while $$\pi$$ captures the effect of the instrument on the treatment. The ratio isolates the effect of the treatment on the outcome, accounting for the variation introduced by the instrument. This approach leverages the exogenous variation provided by the instrument to estimate the causal effect of the treatment.


### Sources of instruments in health studies
The biggest challenge in using IV methods is finding a good IV. There are several common sources of IVs for health studies.
- Randomized encouragement trials: One way to study the effect of a treatment when that treatment cannot be controlled is toconduct a randomized encouragement trial. In such a trial, some subjects are randomly chosen to obtain extra encouragement to take the treatment, and the rest of the subjects receive no extra encouragement. For a randomized encouragement trial, the randomized encouragement assignment is a potential IV. The randomized encouragement is independent of unmeasured confounders because it is randomly assigned by the investigators and will be associated with the treatment if the encouragement is effective. For instance, Permutt and Hebel[^4] studied the effect of maternal smoking during pregnancy on an infant's birthweight using a randomized encouragement trial in which some mothers received extra encouragement to stop smlking through a master's level staff providing information, support, practical guidance, and behavioral strategies. The only potential concern with this instrument is that the randomized encouragement might have a direct effect on the outcome and not through the treatment. For example, the encouragemenet could have a direct effect if the staff person providing the encouragement also encouraged expectant mothers to stop drinking alcohol during pregnancy.

- Distance to specialty care: When comparing two treatments, one of which is only provided by specialty care providers and one of which is provided by more general providers, the distance a person lives from the nearest specialty care provider has often been used as an IV. Because distance to a specialty care provider is often associated with socioeconomic characteristics, it will typically be necessary to control for socioeconomic characteristics in order for distance to potenetially be independent of unmeasured confounders. The possibility that distance might have a direct effect because the time it takes to receive treatment affects outcomes needs to be considered in assessing whether distance is a valid IV. For example, several studies have used relative distance to hospitals as an instrument in analyses aimed at estimating the effects on mortality of teratment with invasive cardiac procedures (e.g., cardiac catherization), after myocardial infaction[^5][^6]. Researchers classify each hospital in the study region as a catheterization or noncatheterization hospital on the basis of the presence of a catheterization laboratory or the overall intensity (or volume) of catheterizations. Patietns are assigned a value of the binary instrument based on whether they live closer to a catheterization hospital (making them more likely to receive the procedure) or a noncatheterization hospital. This IV assumes that the relative distance between a patient's residence and a cardiac catheterization hospital predicts treatment choice independently of all characteristics (such as age, socioeconomic status, health status, or use of life-saving medications) that usually confound the relationship between treatment choice and outcome. 

- Regional variation: Regional differences can serve as IVs when treatment patterns vary significantly across regions due to factors like local practice styles, heatlhcare policies, or market share. This type of IV utilizes the natural differences in treatment rates across regions to isolate the causal effect of a treatment on an outcome. For example, Chen et al. (2022)[^7] investigated the relationship between shoulder-related surgeries and health outcomes, medical malpractice, and adverse events. Given that treatment (surgery) could be confounded by factors such as age and underlying health status, the authors used four state-level tort reform rules as instruments. These tort laws were associated with surgery rates, but their enforcement is unlikely to directly affect treatment outcomes like death or complications, apart form their effect on the choice of treatment in specific clinical areas.

 
- Preference-based IVs: A general strategy for finding an IV for comparing two treatments is to look for naturally occurring variation in medical practice patterns at the level of hospital or individual physician. For instance, Brookhart et al. (2006)[^8] studied the benefits and risks of selective cyclooxygenase 2 (COX-2) inhibitors versus nonselective nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAID) for treating gastrointestinal problems using individual physicians as the IVs. When using preference-based IVs, it is important to consider that the patient mix may differ between the different groups of providers with different prefernces. This variation can make the preference-based IV invalid unless patient mix is fully controlled for. It is useful to look at whether measured patient risk factors differ between groups of providers with diffrent preferences. If there are measured differences, there are likely to be unmeasured differences as well. Additionally, it is important to consider whether the IV has a direct effect; a direct effect could arise if the group of providers that prefers treatment A treats patients differently in ways other than the treatment under study compared with the providers who prefer treatment B. For instance, Newman et al. (2012)[^9] studied the efficacy of phototherapy for newborns with hyperbilirubinemia and considered the frequency of phototherapy use at the newborn's birth hospital as an IV. However, chart reviews revealed that hospitals that use more phototherapy also have a greater use of infant formula. Consequently the proposed preference-based IV has a direct effect (going to a hospital wiht higher use of phototherapy also means a newborn is more likely to receive infant formula even if the newobrn does not receive phototherapy) and is not valid.
